
Medullary thyroid cancer: a type of <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/thyroid_cancer/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">thyroid cancer</a>. Medullary thyroid <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/cancer/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">cancer</a> originates from the so-called “C-cells” of the thyroid gland, also known as parafollicular cells. These parafollicular cells do not make thyroid hormones like the cells of the thyroid follicles (glandular units); instead, they produce a hormone known as <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/calcitonin/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="pharm">calcitonin</a>. Medullary thyroid <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/cancer/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">cancers</a> account for only about 1%-2% of all cancers of the thyroid. Medullary thyroid <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/cancer_101_pictures_slideshow/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="slide">cancer</a> frequently spreads to <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/image-collection/lymph_nodes_picture/picture.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="img">lymph nodes</a> and other organs. Up to 25% of cases run in families. The <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/genetic_disease/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">inherited</a> forms can occur together with other endocrine tumors, in syndromes called multiple endocrine neoplasia (MEN) 2A and MEN 2B.
