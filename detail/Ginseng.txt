
Ginseng: <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/ginseng_eleutherococcus_and_panax_sp-oral/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="pharm">Ginseng</a> has been used in Chinese medicine for thousands of years. Today, ginseng" refers to both American (Panax quinquefolius) and Asian or Korean ginseng (Panax ginseng).  These plants have a similar chemical makeup and contain steroid-like components, ginsenosides, which are believed to be the active ingredients.  On the other hand, Siberian ginseng or Eleuthero (Eleutherococcus senticosus), on the other hand, is a completely unrelated plant and without the active ginsenosides. Panax ginseng may improve cognitive performance, aid in <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/diabetes_mellitus/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">diabetes</a> management, and improve <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/erectile_dysfunction_ed_impotence/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">erectile dysfunction</a>.  Studies have found Panax ginseng ineffective for <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/hot_flashes/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">hot flashes</a>, improving mood and athletic performance.  More research is needed concerning <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/depression/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">depression</a>, chronic <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/fatigue/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">fatigue</a>, <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/cancer/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">cancer</a>, colds and <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/influenza/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">flu</a>, bronchitis, <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/aches_pain_fever/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">fever</a>, digestive problems, <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/fibromyalgia_facts/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">fibromyalgia</a> and <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/anemia/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">anemia</a>. Ginseng can cause elevation in blood pressure, <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/headache/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">headache</a>, vomiting, <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/insomnia/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">insomnia</a>, and nose bleeding.  Ginseng can also cause falsely abnormal blood tests for <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/digoxin/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="pharm">digoxin</a> level in persons taking the drug for <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/heart_disease_coronary_artery_disease/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">heart disease</a>. It is unclear whether ginseng may affect female hormones.  Its use in <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/pregnancy/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">pregnancy</a> is not recommended.  Ginseng may affect the action of the normal blood clotting element (platelets).   It should be avoided in patients taking aspirin, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (such as <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/ibuprofen/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="pharm">ibuprofen</a> [Advil, Motrin] or <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/naproxen/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="pharm">naproxen</a> [Aleve]), or medications to prevent blood clotting (anticoagulants) such as <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/warfarin/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="pharm">warfarin</a> (Coumadin). Ginseng may also cause headaches, <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/tremor/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">tremors</a>, nervousness, and sleeplessness. It should be avoided in persons with manic disorder and psychosis.
