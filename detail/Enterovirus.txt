
Enterovirus: A <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/script/main/art.asp?articlekey=5997" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dict">virus</a> that enters the body 
through the <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/script/main/art.asp?articlekey=25976" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dict">gastrointestinal tract</a> and thrives there, often moving on to attack the nervous system. The polioviruses are enteroviruses.<br><br>

Enteroviruses are small viruses that are made of <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/script/main/art.asp?articlekey=5366" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dict">ribonucleic acid</a> (<a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/script/main/art.asp?articlekey=5382" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dict">RNA</a>) and protein. In addition to the three different polioviruses, there are a number of non-<a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/polio_facts/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">polio</a> enteroviruses that can cause disease in humans, including the <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/coxsackie_virus/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">Coxsackieviruses</a> (Coxsackie A viruses and  Coxsackie B viruses),  echoviruses, and  other enteroviruses.</br></br>
