
Fever, puerperal: <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/aches_pain_fever/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">Fever</a> that lasts for more than 24 hours within the first 10 days after a woman has had a baby. Puerperal <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/fever/symptoms.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="sym">fever</a> is due to an infection, most often of the placental site within the uterus. If the infection involves the bloodstream, it constitutes puerperal <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/sepsis/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">sepsis</a>. Puerperal fever has gone by a number of different names including <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/pregnancy_pain_relief_options_for_birth/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">childbirth</a> fever, childbed fever and postpartum fever. In Latin a "puerpera" is a woman in <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/7_childbirth_and_delivery_methods/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">childbirth</a> since "puer" means child and "parere" means to give birth. The puerperium is the time immediately after the <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/labor_and_delivery/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="proc">delivery of a baby</a>.
