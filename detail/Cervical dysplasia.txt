
Cervical dysplasia: Cellular changes in the cells lining the cervix of the uterus. <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/cervical_dysplasia/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">Cervical dysplasia</a> involves a sequence of cellular changes from mild to severe that are not yet cancerous but constitute the prelude to <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/cervical_cancer/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">cervical cancer</a>.The diagnosis of cervical dysplasia is made from the <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/pap_smear/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="proc">PAP smear</a>. As a rule, cervical dysplasia is found in no more than 5% of PAP smears. The incidence peaks in women 25 to 35 years of age. Risk factors include multiple <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/sexual_sex_problems_in_men/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">sexual</a> partners, the early onset of sexual activity (before age 18), early childbearing (before age 16) and a history of an STD (example chlamydia, genital <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/warts_common_warts/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">warts</a>  gonorrhea,  genital herpes, and  HIV). In a woman with HIV, the speed of changes in cervical dysplasia is accelerated.
