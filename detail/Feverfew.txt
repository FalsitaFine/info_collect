
Feverfew: Tanacetum parthenium (Feverfew) is a member of the sunflower family and has been used for centuries in Europe as a <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/headache/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">headache</a> remedy.  It is most often used today to prevent and treat <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/migraine/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">migraine</a> headaches. <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/feverfew_tanacetum_parthenium-oral/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="pharm">Feverfew</a> can cause allergic reactions, especially in persons who are allergic to ragweed, chrysanthemums, marigolds, or yarrow.   Feverfew may alter the effects of some medications and should be avoided in patients taking medications to prevent blood clotting (anticoagulants) such as <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/warfarin/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="pharm">warfarin</a> (Coumadin). Muscle stiffness, <a href="https://www.medicinenet.com/anxiety/article.htm" onclick="wmdTrack('embd-lnk');" rel="dt">anxiety</a>, and moderate pain can occur following cessation of long-term feverfew use in a syndrome called "post-feverfew syndrome".
